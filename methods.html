Time Management

Pomodoro Technique

Break your work into focused 25-minute intervals separated by short breaks. Developed by Francesco Cirillo, this method fights distraction and mental fatigue.

  1. Pick one task to focus on completely.
  2. Set a timer for 25 minutes and work until it rings.
  3. Take a 5-minute break, stand up, stretch, breathe.
  4. Repeat steps 2-3 four times in a row.
  5. After 4 rounds, take a longer 20-30 minute break.
Best for tasks you keep putting off. The short time box makes starting feel much less intimidating.
Visual Thinking

Mind Mapping

Organise ideas visually by branching out from a central concept. Engages both logical and creative thinking, making connections between topics clearer.

  1. Write the main topic in the centre of a blank page.
  2. Draw branches outward for each major sub-topic.
  3. Add smaller branches for details under each sub-topic.
  4. Use colours, symbols, and keywords - not full sentences.
  5. Review and redraw to consolidate understanding.
Perfect for essay planning, revision overviews, or untangling a confusing topic before diving deeper.
Deep Understanding

The Feynman Technique

Named after Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman, this method forces genuine understanding, not surface familiarity. If you can teach it simply, you truly know it.

  1. Choose a concept you want to understand fully.
  2. Write an explanation as if teaching it to a 12-year-old.
  3. Notice where your explanation breaks down or feels vague.
  4. Go back to your source material and fill those exact gaps.
  5. Simplify further: use analogies, stories, examples.
Use this before exams to expose hidden weaknesses in your knowledge. Struggle to explain a step? That's exactly what you need to re-study.
Long-Term Memory

Spaced Repetition

Review material at increasing intervals over time. Each successful recall pushes the next review further into the future, building strong long-term retention.

  1. Study new material carefully for the first time.
  2. Review it the very next day.
  3. Review again after 3 days, then 7 days, then 2 weeks.
  4. Successful recall? Push the interval out further.
  5. Struggled to recall? Reset the interval and review sooner.
Combine with flashcards for maximum effect. Far more efficient than cramming the night before an exam.
Retrieval Practice

Active Recall

Instead of re-reading notes, actively test yourself on the material. Retrieval practice strengthens memory far more effectively than passive review.

  1. Study the material once, carefully.
  2. Close your notes and everything else.
  3. Write, say, or draw everything you can remember.
  4. Compare against your notes and mark gaps honestly.
  5. Revisit gaps, then repeat the test after a short delay.
After class, spend 5 minutes writing everything you remember from the lecture - no notes. It feels hard because it's working.

Which Method Should I Use?

Not every technique suits every task. Use this as a quick reference guide.

Memorising facts or vocab? Spaced Repetition
Understanding a hard concept? Feynman Technique
Procrastinating on a big task Pomodoro
Revising before an exam? Active Recall
Planning an essay or project? Mind Mapping
Checking if you really know it? Feynman Technique
